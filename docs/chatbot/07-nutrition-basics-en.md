# Gym Tracker — Eating Habits & Nutrition Basics (English)

General, widely accepted healthy-eating education to support training questions. **Rules for the chatbot:** share only the general guidance in this document; never build personalized meal plans, never prescribe calories or exact macros for a specific person, never advise on diets for medical conditions, medication, or weight-loss drugs. Individual needs vary a lot — for anything personal, the right referral is a registered dietitian or doctor. If a user describes very restrictive eating, guilt around food, or binge patterns, respond with empathy and gently recommend speaking to a health professional; never assist with extreme dieting.

## The balanced plate — the everyday default

A simple, general model for most meals:
- **About half the plate:** vegetables and/or fruit, varied colors.
- **About a quarter:** a protein source (chicken, fish, eggs, lean meat, dairy, tofu, legumes).
- **About a quarter:** carbohydrates, preferably whole versions (rice, potatoes, oats, whole-grain bread/pasta, corn tortillas, beans).
- **Plus:** a little healthy fat (olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds) and water as the drink.

Mostly whole, minimally processed foods; treats fit in moderation. Consistency across the week matters far more than any single meal.

## Protein — the training nutrient

- Protein repairs and builds muscle; people who train benefit from more than sedentary people.
- **General published ranges (not a prescription):** the general adult baseline is about 0.8 g per kg of body weight per day, and sports-nutrition guidance commonly cites roughly **1.2–2.0 g per kg per day** for people doing regular strength training. Where someone should land depends on their body, goals, and health — a dietitian can personalize it.
- Spreading protein across 3–4 meals (a palm-sized portion or more per meal) is an easy general habit.
- Good sources: eggs, chicken, turkey, fish, lean beef and pork, milk, yogurt (Greek especially), cheese, tofu, tempeh, lentils, beans, chickpeas, edamame. Combining legumes with grains (beans + rice) rounds out plant proteins.

## Carbohydrates — fuel for workouts

- Carbs are the main fuel for lifting and cardio; very low-carb eating usually makes hard training feel harder.
- Prefer whole sources most of the time: oats, rice, potatoes, whole-grain bread and pasta, fruit, beans and lentils, corn.
- A meal or snack with carbs 1–3 hours before training is a common, sensible habit if training on an empty stomach feels bad.

## Fats — needed, choose mostly the good ones

- Fat supports hormones and overall health; extremely low-fat diets aren't a good general idea.
- Emphasize unsaturated sources: olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish (salmon, sardines).
- Keep heavily processed and fried foods occasional rather than daily.

## Vegetables and fruit

- General target echoed by health organizations: **around five portions a day** of vegetables and fruit combined, the more varied and colorful the better.
- Frozen and canned (in water, low salt/sugar) count — they're convenient and nutritious.
- Easy habit: add one vegetable or fruit to every meal you already eat.

## Hydration

- Water is the default drink. Drink regularly through the day and keep water at hand during workouts (sips between sets or rounds).
- Drink more in heat, heavy-sweat sessions, and long cardio.
- Sugary drinks are best kept occasional; alcohol works against recovery and sleep — general moderation advice applies.
- Personal hydration needs with medical conditions or medications → doctor.

## Eating around workouts — general pattern

- **Before:** something light with carbs and some protein 1–3 hours before if you need it (e.g. yogurt with fruit, a sandwich, oatmeal). Training fasted is fine for some people and awful for others — personal preference.
- **After:** a normal meal with protein and carbs within a few hours. There's no magic 15-minute window for most people — the whole day's eating matters more.
- Long or intense sessions simply mean the day needs a bit more food and water overall.

## Everyday habits that add up

- Regular meal times beat chaotic grazing for most people.
- Cook at home when you can; restaurant and delivery portions run larger and saltier.
- Don't demonize any single food — an "80% whole foods, 20% flexible" pattern is sustainable and effective.
- Sleep and stress strongly affect appetite and cravings; a rough week of sleep often looks like a "willpower" problem but isn't.
- Big picture: the eating pattern you can keep for years beats the perfect diet you quit in three weeks — same principle as training consistency.

## Weight goals — general concept only

Body weight responds to overall energy balance over time: eating more than the body uses leads to gaining, less leads to losing. That concept is general knowledge; **turning it into a personal plan (calories, deficits, timelines) is dietitian/doctor territory, and the chatbot must not do it.** Crash diets and extreme restriction commonly backfire and hurt training. For anyone wanting a weight-change plan, recommend a registered dietitian — and for teens, pregnancy, or any health condition, that referral is mandatory, not optional.

## Supplements

Food first — no supplement is required to make progress. The chatbot does not recommend, dose, or compare supplements (including protein powder, creatine, pre-workouts, or vitamins); questions about them go to a doctor, pharmacist, or registered dietitian, especially with any medication or condition in the picture.

## When to always refer to a professional

Personalized meal plans; weight-loss or weight-gain programs; diabetes, blood pressure, kidney, digestive or any medical condition; food allergies and intolerances; pregnancy and breastfeeding; minors; disordered-eating signs; athletes with competition-specific needs.
