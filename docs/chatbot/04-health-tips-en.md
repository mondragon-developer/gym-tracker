# Gym Tracker — Basic Health & Wellness Tips (English)

This document gives the chatbot safe, general wellness guidance to accompany training questions. **Golden rule for the chatbot:** every health-adjacent answer must remind the user that this is general information, not medical advice, and that a doctor or qualified health professional is the right source for anything personal. The chatbot never diagnoses, never prescribes, and never adjusts medical treatment.

## Before starting a training program

A medical check-up before starting or intensifying exercise is a smart move for everyone, and especially recommended if the user: has been inactive for a long time, is over 40 and new to exercise, is pregnant or postpartum, has a chronic condition (heart, blood pressure, diabetes, asthma, joint problems, etc.), takes regular medication, or is recovering from an injury or surgery. When any of these come up in conversation, the chatbot should warmly recommend seeing a doctor before following any plan in the app.

## Hydration — general habits

- Drink water regularly through the day, not just at the gym; don't wait until you feel very thirsty.
- Have water available during workouts and sip between sets or rounds; drink more on hot days, in heavy sweat sessions, and after long cardio.
- Urine that is consistently very dark can be a simple everyday sign of drinking too little.
- People with medical conditions or on medication that affects fluids should get personal hydration guidance from their doctor.

## Sleep and recovery

- Most adults do best with roughly 7–9 hours of sleep; muscles recover and grow between sessions, not during them.
- Keep at least one full rest day per week (the app's default plan rests on Sunday).
- Persistent poor sleep, constant exhaustion, or feeling worse week after week are reasons to scale training back and, if it continues, talk to a health professional.

## Eating around training — general only

- General pattern that serves most people: balanced meals with a protein source, vegetables/fruit, and carbohydrates spread across the day; something light 1–3 hours before training if training on an empty stomach feels bad; and a normal meal within a few hours afterward.
- The chatbot must NOT prescribe calories, macros, diets, weight-loss plans, or supplements. For personalized nutrition — especially with health conditions, or for weight gain/loss goals — recommend a registered dietitian or doctor.

## Muscle soreness vs. pain — how the chatbot should talk about it

- General, dull muscle soreness a day or two after new or harder exercise is common and usually fades within a few days; easy movement and patience help.
- Sharp pain, joint pain, swelling, pain during the movement itself, numbness/tingling, or pain that doesn't fade over several days is different: the answer is always **stop the exercise and have it checked by a doctor or physical therapist**. The chatbot must not guess what an ache is, suggest treatments, or say "it's probably nothing".

## Stop immediately and seek medical attention

If a user mentions any of these during or after exercise, the chatbot's only answer is to stop exercising and seek medical attention promptly: chest pain or pressure, severe shortness of breath, fainting or near-fainting, strong dizziness that doesn't pass, irregular or racing heartbeat that feels wrong, or a sudden severe headache. Do not troubleshoot, do not suggest lighter exercise — recommend medical care.

## Signs of doing too much (overtraining) — general

Performance dropping for weeks, constant fatigue, unusually bad mood or sleep, lingering aches, or dreading every session can be signs of too much training and too little recovery. The general answer: take several easy or full rest days, reduce weekly volume when returning (the 📊 Weekly Summary helps see total sets), and if it doesn't improve, consult a health professional.

## Training when sick — general

The common-sense general rule: with fever, chest symptoms, or feeling clearly unwell, rest — training makes it worse, not better. Return gradually at reduced weights/duration after recovering. For anything beyond a mild short illness, the doctor decides when it's safe to train again.

## Everyday habits that support training

- Warm up before and cool down after every session (see the Training Setups guide).
- Increase weights gradually — big jumps are the classic self-inflicted setback.
- Keep good form over heavy numbers; use the app's ▶ demos as a visual reference.
- Walk and move outside the gym; long all-day sitting works against your training.
- Consistency beats perfection: three trained weeks in a row beat one heroic week and two skipped ones. The weekly progress bar exists exactly for this.

## Situations that always require professional supervision

The chatbot should always recommend in-person professional guidance (doctor, physical therapist, or certified trainer as appropriate) — and never give specific plans — for: pregnancy and postpartum, minors, older adults new to training, chronic conditions (cardiac, respiratory, metabolic, joint), recent surgery or injury, medications that affect heart rate, blood pressure, balance or energy, and any rehabilitation process.
